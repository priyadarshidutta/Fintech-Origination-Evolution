M-Pesa:
The Business Model

A detailed walkthrough of how M-Pesa works, how it makes money, and why its model became a template for Fintech platforms worldwide. To be read alongside Session 2 classroom materials.

The Origin

Not a Bank. Not a Startup. A Mobile Network That Noticed Something.

Kenya · 2007

M-Pesa was launched in Kenya in 2007 by Safaricom, the country's dominant mobile network operator. It did not begin as a financial product. It began as an observation.

Safaricom noticed that its customers were already transferring mobile airtime credit to each other informally. Airtime was being used as a commodity — it had a known, stable value, everyone with a phone could receive it, and it could be sold to a neighbour for cash. That cash could then be used to buy food, pay rent, or send home to family.

A Real Example

Person A in Nairobi buys 500 shillings of airtime credit and transfers it to Person B — her mother in a village 300km away. B cannot use airtime for food. So B sells that airtime to a neighbour at a small discount — perhaps 450 shillings worth of cash. B then uses that cash to buy food.

It worked. But it had leakage — B lost 50 shillings in the discount. And B had to find a willing buyer first. It was a workaround born out of necessity, not design.

Safaricom asked a simple question: what if we formalised this? Same phone. Same network. Same trust people already had in Safaricom. But instead of routing money through an airtime workaround, let people send money directly — instantly, in full, with no discount and no search for a buyer.

That was M-Pesa. And it worked not because it introduced people to a new institution — but because it delivered a financial service through a channel they already used and already trusted.

Key Insight

M-Pesa did not ask people to become banking customers. It asked them to use their mobile phone — which they already did — to solve a problem they already had.

The People

Meet A, B and C

Three people. One transaction. The whole model.

To understand how M-Pesa works and how it makes money, follow one transaction through three people.

A
Person A
Sender · Lives in Nairobi
Works as a domestic worker in Nairobi. Earns a weekly wage. Wants to send 500 shillings to her mother in a rural village. Has a Safaricom mobile phone. No bank account.
B
Person B
Receiver · Lives in a village 300km away
A's mother. Lives in a rural village. Has a basic mobile phone on the Safaricom network. No bank account. No way to receive money electronically — until M-Pesa.
C
Person C
M-Pesa Agent · Local shopkeeper
Runs a small shop in B's village. Registered as an M-Pesa agent. Keeps a float of cash and M-Pesa credit. Earns a small commission on every transaction he processes.
How It Works

Following One Transaction

A sends 500 shillings to B

Here is exactly what happens when A wants to send B 500 shillings.

1
A deposits cash with a local M-Pesa agent near her workplace
A hands over 500 shillings cash. The agent loads 500 shillings of M-Pesa credit onto A's SIM card. A now has a digital balance of 500 shillings on her phone. M-Pesa charges A a small transaction fee — say 10 shillings — at this point or when she sends.
↓
2
A sends 500 shillings to B's phone number via SMS
A types B's phone number and the amount into the M-Pesa menu on her phone and confirms. Within seconds, B receives an SMS confirming she has received 500 shillings of M-Pesa credit. The transaction takes under a minute.
↓
3
B walks to C's shop and withdraws 500 shillings cash
B shows C the SMS confirmation. C checks the M-Pesa system, hands B 500 shillings cash from his till, and B's M-Pesa balance goes to zero. C's M-Pesa digital balance goes up by 500 shillings — which he can use to replenish his agent float.
What Just Happened

Money moved from Nairobi to a rural village in under a minute. No bank. No branch. No account required. No discount. B received the full 500 shillings. The only cost was the small transaction fee paid by A.

The same journey that once required an informal airtime workaround — with leakage and uncertainty — now takes 60 seconds and costs a fraction of the alternatives.

The Business Model

How M-Pesa Makes Money

Three revenue streams · One elegant cost structure

M-Pesa has three sources of revenue. Each is simple individually. Together they create a business that is highly profitable, capital-light, and extremely difficult to replicate.

🛣
Transaction Fee
The Toll Road Model
Every transfer, withdrawal, bill payment, or airtime purchase on M-Pesa attracts a small fee. The fee is tiered — larger transactions pay a slightly higher fee — but always modest in absolute terms. On a 500 shilling transfer, the fee might be 10 shillings (2%).
10 shillings per transaction × 10 million daily transactions = 100 million shillings per day in fees alone. The road is free to build once. After that, every user pays to drive on it.
💰
The Float
Earning on Other People's Money
When A deposits 500 shillings with the agent, that physical cash flows into a central pooled bank account held by Safaricom. It sits there until B withdraws it at C's shop. At any given moment, millions of people have balances on their phones they have not yet withdrawn. In aggregate this pool is enormous.
Imagine 50 billion shillings sitting in Safaricom's trust account on any given day. At 5% annual interest, that earns 2.5 billion shillings per year — quietly, without a single additional transaction. A and B earn nothing on their balance. They never expected to — they came for the transfer service.
🏪
The Agent Network
A Branch Network at Near-Zero Cost
This is M-Pesa's cost structure, not a revenue stream — but it is what makes the model extraordinary. Instead of building bank branches, M-Pesa recruited local shopkeepers as agents. C earns a small commission per transaction. He self-funds his own cash float. He is motivated to join because it brings customers into his shop.
M-Pesa's result: a national network of cash deposit and withdrawal points, built at almost no fixed capital cost. The cost is entirely variable — M-Pesa only pays when a transaction happens. Traditional banks spend billions building branch networks. M-Pesa outsourced that entirely to entrepreneurs already embedded in their communities.
The Lesson

Why This Model Became a Template

From Kenya to the World

M-Pesa proved three things that every subsequent Fintech platform has borrowed, consciously or not.

Start with one high-frequency, painful, simple problem. Not a full product suite. Not a vision for the future of finance. One thing: A needs to send B 500 shillings. Solve that completely. Build trust. Then expand.

Use infrastructure people already trust. M-Pesa did not ask Kenyans to download an app, open a bank account, or trust a new institution. It used the mobile phone — already in everyone's pocket — and Safaricom's existing network relationship. The product arrived through a channel that required no new leap of faith.

Once the platform exists, layer products on top. After establishing trust through simple money transfer, M-Pesa expanded into savings (M-Shwari), overdrafts (Fuliza), and merchant payments. Each new product cost almost nothing to distribute because millions of people were already on the platform. The hard work — building the agent network, earning trust, acquiring users — had already been done.

The Numbers

Within three years of launch, M-Pesa had more customers than Kenya's entire formal banking system combined. By some estimates, transactions equivalent to 40–50% of Kenya's GDP flow through M-Pesa annually. No single private financial platform in a developed market comes close to that level of economic penetration relative to the size of the economy.

M-Pesa built a toll road for money between people who had never had one. It charged a small fee on every journey, earned quietly on the money sitting between journeys, and used local shopkeepers as its branch network — all without owning a single bank.
